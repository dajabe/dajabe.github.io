Neuroplasticity

You might ask what neuroplasticity is if you are unfamiliar with the concept. If you look up a definition in a dictionary you will get something like the following.

Neuroplasticity:
the ability of the brain to form and reorganize synaptic connections, especially in response to learning or experience or following injury.
This may seem a little intimidating at first but understanding this concept has been shown to improve your learning and can be broken down into simple language. The core of this is that your brain is changeable and capable of growth and learning. As you grown and learn your brain rewires itself to perform the new tasks with greater speed and ability.

Great what does that mean for us? Conventional understanding has us view ourselves as either capable or not capable of certain tasks. For example you might say "I'm no good at math" or "I could never juggle". Neuroplasticity teaches us that while we may not yet be great at math and we may not be capable of juggling; with proper focus, training, and dedication we can reconfigure our brains to be capable of these tasks.

Synapses are pathways in our brain and everytime we use our brains to perform a task we either reinforce existing pathways or create new ones. Our brains are designed to be efficient as well however so when we stop using certain pathways they will fade. This is commonly refered to as being "rusty" when we come back to something we haven't done in a while. This really highlights the importance of practice when it comes to learning new skills. Taking the time to practice the new tasks we are learning helps reinforce the synapses or pathways that makes it possible for our brains to perform the task. Frequent and consistent practice is the key to building new skills and growing in competance.

You might have guessed the neuroplasticity is something that can be measured. If it can be measured does this mean the value of it is fixed for an individual? No it too is something you can train and work on. There are a few different ways you can go about doing this. Healthline has a great article on this topic which goes over different activies that can improve your neuroplasticity. The key take away here is that neuroplasticity is improved when you spend time doing new and novel things. Games are often great for this as they have a variety of different puzzles and ways of thinking in order to do well. Artistic pursuits are also great for increasing neuroplasticity as they encourage different methods of expression. Which is why learning languages and travelling are great for your minds flexiblity as well. The more you form new pathways the better your brain will be at adapting to new skills and scenarios.

Growth Mindset

I view the Growth Mindset as a natural follow on from learning about neuroplasticity. I think you can sum the two mindsets up like this:

Fixed Mindset
I am what I am and there is no changing that.
Growth Mindset
What I will be tomorrow can be changed through hard work and effort.
With a growth mindset you understand that your brain is designed to adapt. If you put yourself in new situations and practice you will see tangible change and growth in your new direction. Carol Dweck has a TED talk exploring growth mindset and fixed mindest. This fundamentally comes down to your own belief. If you believe you can't change then why would you put in the effort to change and so you will stay the same. If you believe you can change and you then put in the work to change you will change.

This is a concept that along with neuroplasticity I learned about in my early 20s and is something that I take seriously in my personal life. I'm always on the look out for fixed ways of thinking and adjusting to a growth mindset when if I catch myself thinking in this way.

What does this change?

For me this doesn't change much as I have already been pointed in this direction of thinking. If you are reading this however and you feel stuck about what you are and where you are in life I challenge you to research these topics further. I have grown so much and I will continue to grow and evolve throughout my life using these principles.

Knowing about something and implimenting it are two different things. The concepts are fairly simple but the execution is hard. You will have times when you slip back into a fixed mindset and it's important not to judge yourself unfairly for this. Just patiently pick yourself back up an and point yourself in the desired direction and keep going. You've got this.
